# Historical Audit of Vertical Slice Content

This document audits the characters, buildings, weapons, religions, institutions, and events featured in the *Reval Rebel* vertical slice. Every reviewed claim includes a source note and one confidence label from the canon vocabulary, maintaining the boundaries between verifiable history, plausible composites, folklore, and invented narrative.

## Confidence Labels
- **`attested`**: A verifiable historical fact, person, or term present in primary sources or established historiography.
- **`plausible composite`**: A fictional element built strictly from historically accurate archetypes, structures, or plausible events.
- **`folklore`**: An element drawn from period-appropriate or regionally appropriate myth, belief, or oral tradition, presented subjectively within the game.
- **`invented`**: Entirely fictional content created to serve the game's specific narrative, dramatic, or gameplay needs, restricted by the boundaries of the established history.

  * Label: **`invented`**

  * Label: **`invented`**
  * Source Note: Fictional character created to act as a bridge between the rural Estonian rebellion and the city's inhabitants.
* **Captain Henning (Commander of Viru Watch)** 
  * Label: **`plausible composite`**
  * Source Note: Fictional composite representing the Danish or Low German watch authority. The name "Henning" and the existence of a city watch commander are historically plausible for 14th-century Reval.
* **Jürgen Witte (Hanseatic Merchant)** 
  * Label: **`plausible composite`**
  * Source Note: Represents the Hanseatic League merchants who dominated Reval's trade. The name and occupation are historically grounded composites of typical merchant class individuals.

  * Label: **`plausible composite`**
  * Source Note: A baptized native bridging Christian and older traditions. Midwives were common, and syncretism (mixing Catholic rites with older oral traditions and songs) is well-documented in the region.

  * Label: **`plausible composite`**
  * Source Note: Standard guards protecting gates and enforcing the law under Danish/Livonian authority in 14th-century Reval.

## 2. Buildings and Locations

* **Lower-Town Street and Well**
  * Label: **`attested`**
  * Source Note: The lower town of Reval (Tallinn) and its public wells are archaeologically and historically verified features of the 14th-century city.
* **Smith's Forge**
  * Label: **`plausible composite`**
  * Source Note: Blacksmith workshops were essential, ubiquitous infrastructure in medieval towns. 
* **Aita's Brewery**
  * Label: **`plausible composite`**
  * Source Note: Small-scale brewing was a common domestic and commercial activity in medieval European towns.
* **Watch Checkpoint / Detention Yard**
  * Label: **`plausible composite`**
  * Source Note: The Viru Gate (built in the 14th century) and other city defenses had guard posts, holding areas, and watchmen.
* **Town Hall and Market Square (1343 state)**
  * Label: **`attested`**
  * Source Note: Tallinn Town Hall is first mentioned in 1322 and its building history reaches the late 13th century. The current medieval appearance and tower date to 1402-1404, so the 1343 model uses the earlier elongated limestone hall with a tile roof and omits the later tower. Sources: [Tallinn Town Hall - The building](https://raekoda.tallinn.ee/en/the-building), [Medieval Heritage - Tallinn Town Hall](https://medievalheritage.eu/en/main-page/heritage/estonia/tallinn-town-hall).
* **Holy Spirit Chapel and Almshouse (1343 state)**
  * Label: **`attested`**
  * Source Note: The chapel is indirectly recorded in 1316 beside its almshouse; its larger Gothic rebuilding belongs mainly to the later 14th century. The 1343 model therefore uses a modest chapel-almshouse complex and omits both the later tower and the seventeenth-century public clock. Source: [Medieval Heritage - Holy Spirit Church](https://medievalheritage.eu/en/main-page/heritage/estonia/tallinn-holy-spirit-church-puha-vaimu-kirik).
* **Building Materials Mix (limestone, log, plank, plaster, limited brick)**
  * Label: **`attested`** for the material families; **`plausible reconstruction`** for any map-level proportions
  * Source Note: Local limestone (paekivi) supplied walls, churches, civic work and an increasing number of burgher houses. Archaeology in the NUKU quarter records a mid-14th-century round-log auxiliary building and a modest limestone house only in the fourth quarter / end of that century; the broader building survey describes wooden and stone buildings coexisting throughout the 14th century. No reviewed source supplies a defensible citywide 1343 wood-to-stone percentage. Maps must therefore vary the mix by institution, wealth and position rather than claiming that either material dominated every district. Brick is allowed only as a limited detail until site-specific evidence supports more. Sources: [Heinloo, Archaeological investigations in the NUKU courtyard](https://arheoloogia.ee/ave2013/AVE2013_06_Heinloo_Nuku.pdf), [Tallinn residential buildings](https://medievalheritage.eu/en/main-page/heritage/estonia/tallinn-gothic-houses/), [Visit Tallinn - Lasnamäe limestone](https://www.visittallinn.ee/eng/visitor/ideas-tips/tips-and-guides/lasnam%C3%A4e).
  * Visual decision (2026-07-21): ordinary 1343 buildings may use horizontal log, plank, restrained half-timber or lime-plastered timber construction; affluent/institutional buildings may use limestone. Do not copy later surviving Gothic stone facades wholesale, and do not treat decorative diagonal Fachwerk as a default Reval motif.
* **Roof Covers (wooden shingle, reed/straw thatch, ceramic tile)**
  * Label: **`plausible reconstruction`**; exact 1343 district percentages remain **`unknown`**
  * Source Note: Clay tile is directly supported on the early limestone Town Hall and major ecclesiastical buildings, while timber, shingle and thatch are regionally plausible for ordinary and service structures. The reviewed sources do not quantify roof-cover shares in 1343 Reval. P0-072 therefore defines broad, reviewable production ranges rather than presenting a measured distribution; tile is concentrated on civic, religious and wealthy stone buildings, with shingle/thatch on ordinary timber and service buildings unless a map-specific source overrides it.
* **Reval district silhouette and Toompea relief (1343-inspired layout)**
  * Label: **`plausible reconstruction`**
  * Source Note: Tallinn's official history places the later town-wall circuit in the 14th century, while the heritage authority identifies the preserved medieval street/property network as a defining Old Town value. The district map therefore uses an irregular, stepped southern wall rather than a modern rectangle, keeps a denser southern ward, and separates the broad monastery/guild precinct from the merchant ward toward the coast. Toompea is a distinct fortified hill and is rendered as a wider plateau elevated above the Lower Town. The Workers, Knights, Merchant, Monastery, Trade Harbour, and Fishing Harbour labels are gameplay-facing functional names, not claimed medieval administrative district names. Sources: [History of Tallinn](https://www.tallinn.ee/en/history-tallinn), [Tallinn heritage protection](https://www.tallinn.ee/en/ehitus/heritage-protection), [Toompea wall restoration notice](https://www.tallinn.ee/en/news/patkuli-stairs-closed-due-restoration-toompea-wall-until-27-june).

## Bishop's Garden chronology (1343 map boundary review)

The Archbishop's Garden gameplay region uses the archaeologically located Bishop's Garden on the western side of the Toompea plateau. Reppo's excavation report (`history/AVE2018_12_Reppo_Toompea.pdf`) records the late medieval Bishop's House only from 1420, so the 1343 map includes the garden plot and medieval well but does not present that later house as contemporary. This site is distinct from Danish King's Garden by Lühike Jalg.

### How to use this dossier

This dossier sets bounded environment targets for later art, composition, and audit work. It does not claim that a complete measured plan of Reval survives from 1343. It also does not activate any prototype map.

Evidence classes used below supplement the canon labels at the top of this document:

- **`A - attested`**: contemporary documentary statement, dated fabric, or archaeology that directly supports the decision.
- **`B - bounded reconstruction`**: a production range inferred from several attested observations. The range is a testable authoring hypothesis, not a measured 1343 statistic.
- **`C - later survival analogy`**: a later medieval building or surviving Old Town feature used only for general form. It may not be copied as the 1343 state.
- **`D - gameplay invention`**: a fictional assignment or composition required by the game. It must remain inside the physical limits established by A-C evidence.
- **`U - unknown`**: reviewed evidence does not establish a useful 1343 answer. The map must choose the least specific reversible treatment and retain the label.

The numeric ranges below use these definitions:

- **Built footprint** is the horizontal footprint of roofed buildings on developable land. It excludes water, non-developable cliff, perimeter walls, freestanding plot walls, piers, and temporary stalls.
- **Open space** is the complement of built footprint on developable land and includes streets, yards, closes, gardens, and service ground.
- **Ground surfaces** are shares of traversable unbuilt land after water is excluded. Select values within each row so the authored surface shares total 100%. `Stone` includes cobble, pebble/rubble pavement, and local limestone paving.
- Ranges are intentionally broad. They are invalid as automated pass/fail thresholds until the human review record at the end of this section is signed.

### Shared 1343 constraints

1. **Street and property structure - A/B/C.** Preserve the inherited irregular street directions and narrow plot rhythm where the retained maps overlap the medieval core (H01-H04). Use the 7-11 m strip-plot width only as a merchant-quarter median band. Artisan, institutional, edge, and harbour plots may be wider, shorter, or irregular. Modern streetscape photographs and later cadastral plans are alignment evidence, not permission to reproduce later facades.
2. **Density - B.** Dense frontage means frequent street-facing buildings plus rear yards and service structures, not blanket roof coverage. Institutional closes, hill compounds, wall margins, wet ground, and harbour work areas are intentionally more open. Each map has its own range below.
3. **Ordinary architecture - A/B.** Timber and stone coexist in 1343 (H04-H05). Ordinary houses and service buildings may be log, plank, restrained half-timber, or lime-plastered timber over timber/stone sills. Limestone concentrates in walls, churches, civic work, affluent merchant houses, cellars, wells, drains, and selected workshops. Do not use later Tallinn Gothic merchant facades on every property.
4. **Exceptional architecture - A/U.** A named landmark receives its dated 1343 mass, not its best-known later silhouette. If the state is unknown, use a low-detail placeholder mass and preserve the stable landmark anchor for later review.
5. **Roof covers - B/U.** Use tile principally on attested or reconstructed civic, religious, defensive, and wealthy stone buildings. Use wood shingle and thatch/reed for most ordinary timber/service roofs. Exact district shares are unknown; the map-specific bands below are production hypotheses.
6. **Ground - A/B.** Paved routes existed, including flat limestone, erratic/rubble stone and pebble road surfaces (H04, H09-H10). This supports paving of primary routes and important closes, not a blanket cobblestone ground fill. Secondary lanes, yards and exterior roads use packed earth, sand, chips, dung-darkened soil and seasonally muddy wear; grass survives in gardens, margins and lightly used ground.
7. **Drainage - A/B/U.** Shape streets and yards to shed water toward open ditches, wall moats, coast, wells/soakage points, or localized stone/wood gutters. Covered stone drainage is allowed where attested, especially at the Coastal Gate (H11). Do not infer a citywide underground sewer from later timber pipes (H09).
8. **Fences and plot edges - B/C.** Use timber boards, wattle, hedges, service-building edges, and limited limestone plot walls. The later-medieval 3 m wall described for wealthy strip plots (H04) is an upper-bound analogy, not the default for ordinary 1343 yards.
9. **Vegetation species - A/B/U.** Retain fruit and kitchen planting in monastic and selected household plots (H05, H15), orchard/garden use on western Toompea where H12 supports the land use, wet-margin reeds/scrub, and sparse disturbed-ground plants in compact streets. H17 confirms the value and presence of medieval urban plant remains but also documents uneven sampling. Exact 1343 species proportions are therefore **U**. [`FLORA_FAUNA.md`](FLORA_FAUNA.md) is a provisional implementation palette, not independent historical evidence.
10. **Garden and agricultural land use - A/B/U.** Garden, orchard, pasture, meadow, field-strip, and fallow shares below are bounded composition hypotheses. Monastic/household gardens and some crop use are supported in kind (H05, H15, H17), but no reviewed source supplies map-level acreage for 1343. Keep all food production physically plausible, reversible, and subordinate to attested routes and institutions.
11. **Fauna presence bands - B/U.** `none` means no resident authored placement; `trace` means a transient/scavenger or at most one small context-specific group; `low` means one small pen, tether, working group, or recurring ambient cluster; `moderate` means several small controlled groups without blanket scatter. H18-H19 support livestock and fowl in medieval Estonia, but consumption remains do not locate live animals. Wild mammals remain `none` inside dense urban maps and at most `trace` on signed outer margins; no predator may occupy a required route.
12. **Topography - A/B.** Toompea is a high limestone plateau; the Lower Town lies at its foot; the Coastal Gate sits above lower harbour ground; southern and eastern edges include low/wet coastal terrain (H01, H08, H10-H12). Maps must express local slope and drainage rather than flattening every district. Terrain scale is a gameplay abstraction and must not be labelled a measured elevation model.
